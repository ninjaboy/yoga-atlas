📖 Foundational Texts

The source literature, oldest to newest. The dating of almost every pre-modern yoga text is contested — colophons are late, manuscripts are few, and attributions are traditional rather than documentary. Dates below carry ⚠️ where the scholarship is genuinely unsettled. Many texts are freely readable on Wikisource, Archive.org and sacred-texts.com; verified open links are in Open Media and the ## Sources section.

Pre-classical & philosophical

Text Date Attribution Why it matters
Vedas c. 1500–500 BCE anonymous, oral ritual + hymns; the seedbed of ascetic/meditative ideas
Upaniṣads (esp. Kaṭha, Śvetāśvatara) c. 800–200 BCE various first philosophical use of yoga; self (ātman), breath, meditation
Bhagavad Gītā c. 200 BCE part of the Mahābhārata the three paths — karma, bhakti, jñāna (Paths and Lineages)

The Kaṭha Upaniṣad gives the famous chariot image of the senses and the earliest clear statement that yoga is the steady control (sthira) of the senses; the Śvetāśvatara adds posture, breath and a theistic colouring. These feed directly into Philosophy and Concepts.

Classical yoga

Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali

  • Date: ⚠️ early centuries CE — estimates range from 2nd c. BCE to 4th–5th c. CE; most scholars now favour roughly 325–425 CE, but this is genuinely unverifiable.
  • Author: the sage Patañjali, who compiled and systematised rather than invented — drawing on Sāṃkhya metaphysics, Buddhist meditation vocabulary, and older ascetic yoga. ⚠️ Whether this Patañjali is the grammarian of the same name is disputed and probably wrong.
  • Form: 196 terse aphorisms (some editions count 195) in four pādas. Defines yoga as citta-vṛtti-nirodha — the stilling of the mind's fluctuations — and lays out the eight limbs (aṣṭāṅga); see Philosophy and Concepts. Āsana here means only a steady, comfortable seat (sthira-sukham āsanam), nothing of the posture-system later associated with the word (Practices).

The four pādas:

Pāda ~Sūtras Theme Contents
I. Samādhi-pāda 51 contemplation What is yoga? Definition (citta-vṛtti-nirodha), the five vṛttis, practice (abhyāsa) and dispassion (vairāgya), īśvara-praṇidhāna, types of samādhi (samprajñāta / asamprajñāta)
II. Sādhana-pāda 55 practice Why and how? The kleśas (afflictions), karma, the kriyā-yoga of discipline; the first five (external) limbs — yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra
III. Vibhūti-pāda 56 powers The three internal limbs — dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi (together saṃyama) — and the siddhis (extraordinary powers: knowing past lives, other minds, mastery of the elements) that arise from them, with a warning not to be ensnared
IV. Kaivalya-pāda 34 liberation The nature of mind and karma; kaivalya — the "aloneness" / absolute freedom of pure consciousness (puruṣa) once it is disentangled from matter (prakṛti)

Key commentaries (the layered Sanskrit tradition):

Commentary Author Date Note
Yoga-bhāṣya Vyāsa ⚠️ 4th–5th c. CE the foundational bhāṣya; every later commentary builds on it. Some scholars now argue the sūtras + bhāṣya were a single authored Pātañjala-yogaśāstra
Tattva-vaiśāradī Vācaspati Miśra c. 950 CE the most significant early sub-commentary (gloss on Vyāsa)
Rāja-mārtaṇḍa Bhoja Rāja 11th c. a clear, independent royal commentary
Yoga-vārttika Vijñānabhikṣu 16th c. a Vaiṣṇava reading harmonising Sāṃkhya with Vedānta (bhedābheda)

See Key Figures for the commentators. Open translation: Charles Johnston (1912) on Wikisource; Rāma Prasāda's Vyāsa+Vācaspati edition on Archive.org (links below).

The Haṭha corpus (medieval)

Recent scholarship (above all Mallinson & Singleton, Roots of Yoga, 2017) has rewritten this lineage. Haṭha yoga did not begin with the Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā; the Pradīpikā is a 15th-c. anthology of much older material, and the earliest substantial source now appears to be the Amṛtasiddhi — from a Buddhist, not Śaiva, milieu.

Text Date Author Contents
Amṛtasiddhi ⚠️ c. late 11th c. (Mallinson & Szántó 2021) anon.; invokes Siddha Virūpa the earliest substantial haṭha text, though it never uses the word haṭha; introduces mahāmudrā, bindu preservation, central-channel ascent
Gorakṣa Śataka / Gorakṣa Saṃhitā ⚠️ c. 11th–13th c. attrib. Gorakṣanāth early Nāth haṭha; a six-limbed (ṣaḍaṅga) scheme; nāḍī, cakra, prāṇa, kuṇḍalinī — foundational to the subtle-body model
Śiva Saṃhitā ⚠️ c. 1300–1500 (Mallinson; others say 17th c.) unknown; likely composed near Vārāṇasī non-dual Vedānta framing; prāṇa, nāḍīs, four stages of prāṇāyāma, mudrās, the four kinds of yogin; ~517 verses in 5 chapters
Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā (HYP) 15th c. Svātmārāma the classic manual; 4 chapters, ~389 verses, compiled from ~20 earlier sources
Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā c. 17th c. sage Gheraṇḍa (to disciple Caṇḍa Kāpāli) an "encyclopaedia" of haṭha; the saptāṅga (seven-limb) scheme; 7 chapters, 351 verses

HYP — chapter structure:

Ch. ~Verses Subject
I 67 yama/niyama, then āsanas (the classic ~15 seated/strength postures)
II 78 ṣaṭkarma (six cleansings) + prāṇāyāma (the kumbhakas)
III 130 mudrās & bandhas + kuṇḍalinī
IV 114 samādhi, nāda (inner sound), rāja-yoga as the goal

Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā — the saptāṅga (seven limbs), one per chapter: 1. Ṣaṭkarma — purification (dhauti, basti, neti, nauli/lauliki, trāṭaka, kapālabhāti) 2. Āsana — 32 postures 3. Mudrā — 25 seals/gestures 4. Pratyāhāra — sense-withdrawal 5. Prāṇāyāma — breath-control 6. Dhyāna — meditation 7. Samādhi — absorption

This is a deliberately body-first sequence (it begins with cleansing, not ethics) — a contrast with Patañjali's aṣṭāṅga that is worth noting on Practices and Paths and Lineages.

The HYP, Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā and Śiva Saṃhitā are the three most influential surviving haṭha texts; they're bundled in translations such as The Forceful Yoga (Bhatt/Sinh). The Amṛtasiddhi sits behind all three as their now-recovered ancestor.

Visual / late-medieval source texts

  • Bahr al-ḥayāt ("Ocean of Life", c. 1600–1604) — earliest known illustrated āsana manuscript, a Persian reworking commissioned in the Mughal court (Jahāngīr era).
  • Joga Pradīpikā (1737, attrib. Rāmānandi Jayatarāma) — illustrated āsanas + mudrās; 84 postures.
  • Śrītattvanidhi (19th c., Mysore palace) — 122 illustrated postures, the most elaborate pre-20th-c. visual āsana text; a direct influence on Krishnamacharya's Mysore school → images catalogued in Open Media.

Influential modern books

  • Vivekānanda — Rāja Yoga (1896): re-read the Yoga Sūtras for a modern Western audience and largely defined "meditation = yoga" in the West.
  • B.K.S. Iyengar — Light on Yoga (1966): the modern posture canon.
  • Mallinson & Singleton — Roots of Yoga (2017, Penguin Classics): the scholarly source-anthology that reorganised this whole field; the basis for most ⚠️ flags above.
